#### LESSON 3: Experimental Designs ‚è≥ PLANNED
**Curriculum Mapping:**
- AQA GCSE (p. 100): Three main designs
- AQA A-Level (p. 172): Experimental designs with validity evaluation

**GCSE Content:**
- Independent Groups Design: Different participants in each condition
  - Strength: No order effects
  - Weakness: Need more participants
  - When to use: When participants might remember the study
- Repeated Measures Design: Same participants in all conditions
  - Strength: Fewer participants needed, controls participant variables
  - Weakness: Order effects possible (practice/fatigue)
  - When to use: When you have few participants available
- Matched Pairs Design: Similar participants paired
  - Strength: Some control of participant variables, no order effects
  - Weakness: Time-consuming to match
  - When to use: When designing with participant variables in mind

**A-Level Content (Year 12-13):**
- How design affects statistical analysis (independent vs paired tests)
- Counterbalancing to control order effects (ABBA, randomised)
- Threat to internal validity from participant variables (independent groups)
- Power considerations and participant numbers
- Type I and Type II error implications
- When to use each design based on research question
- Balancing practical constraints with validity

#### LESSON 5: Sampling Methods ‚è≥ PLANNED
**Curriculum Mapping:**
- AQA GCSE (p. 102): Five sampling methods
- AQA A-Level (p. 176): Sampling methods with representativeness evaluation

**GCSE Content:**
- Random Sampling: Everyone has equal chance, unbiased
  - Method: Use random number generator or draw names
  - Strength: Unbiased
  - Weakness: Need population list, may not be representative
- Opportunity Sampling: People who are available, quick but biased
  - Method: Ask volunteers who are available
  - Strength: Quick and easy
  - Weakness: Unrepresentative, biased
- Systematic Sampling: Every nth person
  - Method: Select every 5th person from list
  - Strength: Simple to do, unbiased
  - Weakness: If pattern in population, becomes biased
- Stratified Sampling: Ensure subgroups represented proportionally
  - Method: Identify subgroups, sample proportionally from each
  - Strength: Representative of all groups
  - Weakness: Complex, need to know subgroup sizes
- Volunteer/Convenience Sampling: Quick recruitment method
  - Strength: Easy and fast
  - Weakness: Often unrepresentative (self-selection bias)

**A-Level Content (Year 12-13):**
- Sampling distribution concepts
- Standard error and sample size effects
- Representativeness and generalisability
- Effect of sampling method on validity of conclusions
- Population parameters vs sample statistics
- Statistical inference limitations based on sampling
- Sampling bias and systematic error
- Participant characteristics and external validity

#### LESSON 7: Observational Methods ‚è≥ PLANNED
**Curriculum Mapping:**
- AQA GCSE (p. 108): Observation studies and methods
- AQA A-Level (p. 182-184): Observational techniques and design

**GCSE Content:**
- Naturalistic Observation: Watch in real setting, no changes
  - Strength: Realistic behaviour, high ecological validity
  - Weakness: No control, observer bias
  - Example: Watch children playing in school playground
- Controlled Observation: Some environment control, systematic watching
  - Strength: More control than naturalistic, still fairly realistic
  - Weakness: Less realistic than naturalistic
  - Example: Observe children in special room set up like playground
- Covert Observation: Participants don't know they're watched
  - Strength: No behavior change (Hawthorne effect eliminated)
  - Weakness: Serious ethical issues (privacy violation)
  - Example: One-way mirror observation
- Overt Observation: Participants know they're watched
  - Strength: More ethical
  - Weakness: Observer effect (behavior change due to being watched)
  - Example: Researcher with clipboard in classroom
- Observer Bias: Researcher sees what they expect to see
  - Problem: Distorts what's actually observed
  - Solution: Clear definitions, multiple observers

**A-Level Content (Year 12-13):**
- Observational techniques:
  - Structured observation (predetermined categories)
  - Unstructured observation (description of all behavior)
  - Event sampling (record when specific behavior occurs)
  - Time sampling (record behavior at set time intervals)
- Behavioral categories: observable, non-overlapping, comprehensive
- Inter-observer reliability: agreement between two observers
  - Calculate percentage agreement or Cohen's kappa
  - Higher reliability = more objective observations
- Sampling methods in observation:
  - Continuous recording (record everything)
  - Event sampling (record specific behaviors)
  - Time sampling (record at intervals)
- Participant observation: Researcher joins group, loses objectivity
- Non-participant observation: Maintains objectivity
- Validity trade-offs: naturalistic but less controlled
- Research issues: Demand characteristics, observer effects

**Target Slides: 10** | **Duration: ~80 minutes** | **Status: PLANNED**

#### LESSON 8: Self-Report Methods ‚è≥ PLANNED
**Curriculum Mapping:**
- AQA GCSE (p. 106): Interviews and questionnaires
- AQA A-Level (p. 186-188): Self-report techniques and design

**GCSE Content:**
- Questionnaires: Written questions, can be open or closed
  - Open questions: "Describe your experience" - Rich detail, hard to analyse
  - Closed questions: "Agree/Disagree" - Easy to analyse, may lack depth
  - Design tips: Avoid leading questions ("Don't you think...?"), avoid double-barrelled questions ("Do you like X and Y?")
  - Strength: Quick to distribute, easy to collect large samples
  - Weakness: Low response rates, people may not be truthful
- Interviews: Face-to-face conversations
  - Strength: Detailed information, can clarify answers, see non-verbal behavior
  - Weakness: Hard to analyse, time-consuming, people may not share sensitive info
  - Types: Can be formal or informal
- Designing good questionnaires and interviews:
  - Clear questions
  - Appropriate language
  - Avoid bias

**A-Level Content (Year 12-13):**
- Interview types:
  - Structured: Predetermined questions in fixed order (quantifiable)
  - Semi-structured: Some predetermined questions, flexibility to explore (balanced)
  - Unstructured: Open conversation, researcher guides topic (rich detail)
- Response bias and social desirability (tendency to give socially acceptable answers)
- Validity of self-report: Do people report accurately?
  - Influenced by: Social desirability, memory limitations, interpretation of questions
- Reliability: Can measure consistently over time?
  - Test-retest reliability: Same person, same answer?
- Quantifying qualitative data:
  - Coding: Categorize responses
  - Content analysis: Count occurrences of themes
  - Thematic analysis: Identify patterns
- Questionnaire design details:
  - Likert scales: 1-5 agreement rating
  - Closed vs open questions
  - Question ordering effects
  - Response options
- Interview design:
  - Probing questions (follow up)
  - Prompts (help participant think)
  - Recording and transcription
- Limitations of asking people about behaviour:
  - Social desirability bias
  - Memory limitations (recall bias)
  - Response consistency (acquiescence)
  - Questionnaire issues (misinterpretation, order effects)

#### LESSON 9: Correlations & Data Analysis ‚è≥ PLANNED
**Curriculum Mapping:**
- AQA GCSE (p. 110): Correlations and scatter diagrams
- AQA A-Level (p. 190): Correlations, types of data, statistics, and testing
- AQA A-Level (p. 192-200): Types of data, descriptive statistics, mathematical content, statistical testing

**GCSE Content:**
- Correlation: Association between two variables
- Positive Correlation: Both increase together
  - Example: Height and weight - taller people tend to be heavier
  - Graph: Scatter points go bottom-left to top-right
- Negative Correlation: One increases, other decreases
  - Example: Exercise and body fat - more exercise = less fat
  - Graph: Scatter points go top-left to bottom-right
- Zero Correlation: No relationship
  - Example: Height and IQ - no association
  - Graph: Scatter points scattered randomly
- Scatter Diagrams: Plot the association visually
  - X-axis: First variable (co-variable)
  - Y-axis: Second variable (co-variable)
  - Points show association pattern
- Cannot claim causation with correlation: Association ‚â† Causation
  - Third variable problem: Other factors explain the relationship
  - Example: Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase in summer (temperature causes both)

**A-Level Content (Year 12-13):**
- Types of data:
  - Categorical/Nominal: Groups/categories (e.g., male/female, color)
  - Ordinal: Ranked order (e.g., 1st place, 2nd place, tiny/small/medium/large)
  - Interval: Equal intervals, no true zero (e.g., temperature in Celsius, IQ score)
  - Ratio: Equal intervals, true zero (e.g., height in cm, reaction time in ms)
- Descriptive statistics:
  - Measures of central tendency:
    - Mode: Most frequent value (all data types)
    - Median: Middle value when ordered (ordinal+)
    - Mean: Sum√∑count (interval+ only)
  - Measures of dispersion:
    - Range: Highest - lowest
    - Interquartile range: Middle 50% spread
    - Standard deviation: How spread out from mean
- Pearson's r (correlation coefficient): For continuous data
  - Range: -1 to +1 (perfect negative to perfect positive)
  - Strength: r > 0.7 (strong), 0.4-0.7 (moderate), < 0.4 (weak)
  - Interpretation: Strength and direction of association
- Spearman's rho: Rank-order correlation for ordinal data
  - Similar interpretation to Pearson's r
- Interpreting correlation strength
- Co-variables vs IV/DV terminology
- Correlation vs regression (predicting values)
- Role of correlation in initial research (hypothesis generation)
- Third variable/confounding explanations
- Mathematical content (Year 13):
  - Calculating correlation coefficients
  - Standard deviation formula
  - Mean and variance calculations
- Statistical testing: The sign test (Year 13)
  - Assessing whether results are due to chance or real effect
  - Null hypothesis: No difference/effect
  - Alternative hypothesis: There is a difference/effect
  - Significance level: p < 0.05 typically
  - Type I error: False positive (reject true null)
  - Type II error: False negative (fail to reject false null)
- Presentation of quantitative data:
  - Tables with appropriate statistics
  - Graphs (bar charts, histograms, scatter plots, line graphs)
  - APA format reporting

- AQA Year 13: Correlation coefficients (Pearson's r, Spearman's rho)

**GCSE Content:**
- Correlation: Association between two variables
- Positive Correlation: Both increase together (height and weight)
- Negative Correlation: One increases, other decreases (exercise and body fat)
- Zero Correlation: No relationship
- Scatter Diagrams: Plot the association visually
- Cannot claim causation with correlation (third variable problem)

**A-Level Content:**
- Pearson's r: Correlation coefficient for continuous data (-1 to +1)
- Spearman's rho: Rank-order correlation
- Interpreting correlation strength (weak, moderate, strong)
- Co-variables vs IV/DV terminology
- Correlation vs regression
- Role of correlation in initial research (hypothesis generation)
- Third variable/confounding explanations

**A-Level Content (Year 12-13):**
- Why use statistics? (Assess whether results due to chance)
- Significance level (Œ±) - typically 0.05
- Null hypothesis significance testing (NHST)
- Type I error (reject true null) vs Type II error (fail to reject false null)
- Choosing test based on:
  - Data type (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio)
  - Design (independent, repeated, matched groups)
  - Test purpose (difference vs correlation)
- Common tests:
  - Independent groups difference: Independent t-test (parametric)
  - Repeated measures difference: Paired t-test or Wilcoxon (parametric/non-parametric)
  - Correlation: Pearson's r or Spearman's rho
  - Chi-square for categorical data
- Effect size: Cohen's d (small/medium/large thresholds)
- Meta-analysis: Combining results across studies
- Reporting standards: APA format with statistics
